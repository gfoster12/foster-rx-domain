Regulatory Precedent — Nrf2/Antioxidant Pathway in Neurodegeneration

FDA Action History

PRDX1 is an Nrf2-regulated antioxidant enzyme, situating any PRDX1 modulator program within a broader Nrf2/oxidative stress pathway that has documented FDA precedent. Most directly relevant: omaveloxolone (Skyclarys, Reata/Biogen), an Nrf2 activator, received FDA approval in February 2023 for Friedreich ataxia — the first FDA-approved drug for FA and a meaningful precedent for Nrf2-pathway modulation in a neurodegenerative indication. Approval was based on the Phase 2 MOXIe Part 2 trial showing −2.41 mFARS point benefit vs. placebo at week 48, with orphan drug, fast track, priority review, and rare pediatric disease designations. The omaveloxolone precedent demonstrates FDA receptivity to oxidative stress mechanisms in neurodegeneration when supported by mechanistic biomarker evidence and clinical functional improvement.

Historical AD Antioxidant Trial Outcomes

Two foundational AD antioxidant trials inform the PRDX1 development context. The Sano et al. 1997 ADCS trial of selegiline plus alpha-tocopherol (NEJM 336:1216–22) showed delayed time-to-clinical-endpoint in moderately impaired AD patients, but only after MMSE baseline adjustment — illustrating the design sensitivity of antioxidant AD trials. The Galasko et al. 2012 ADCS antioxidant biomarker trial (Arch Neurol, NCT00117403) showed CSF F2-isoprostane reduction of 19% in the E/C/ALA group at week 16, validating that oxidative stress biomarkers can move with appropriate intervention — but also raised an accelerated cognitive decline safety signal in that arm. The canonical pattern: biomarker engagement is achievable, but cognitive endpoint outcomes require careful design.

Biomarker Endpoint Feasibility

The endpoint stack for a PRDX1 modulator program in AD has three established components: CSF oxidative stress biomarkers (F2-isoprostanes, 8-isoprostane) for engagement assessment; plasma p-tau217 (FDA-accepted as supportive AD biomarker in 2024 guidance) for downstream neurodegeneration anchor; and clinical endpoints following the post-Aduhelm/Leqembi standard — CDR-SB or equivalent global function measure as primary, with biomarker composite supporting evidence. The Aduhelm 2021 AdComm (10–0 against approval, FDA approval over committee, market withdrawal 2024) versus Leqembi 2023 AdComm (6–0 in favor, clean approval) defines the contemporary regulatory standard: agency receptivity is contingent on robust clinical effect plus biomarker support, not biomarker engagement alone.

Complete Response Letter Risk Factors

Predictable CRL risk factors for a PRDX1 modulator program: (1) inadequate clinical effect size on cognitive endpoints despite biomarker engagement (the Aduhelm-pattern risk, sharpened by the Galasko 2012 accelerated-cognitive-decline safety signal in the antioxidant arm); (2) target redundancy and selectivity concerns — the peroxiredoxin family includes six members with partially overlapping function, raising selectivity questions FDA reviewers may probe in CMC and pharmacology review; (3) safety signals from PRDX1’s documented roles in cell proliferation signaling beyond redox homeostasis, requiring careful preclinical characterization of effects on proliferation in non-target tissues; (4) CMC challenges on activator-class chemistry, less established than inhibitor chemistry for review pathway purposes.

TL;DR PRDX1 regulatory pathway has meaningful precedent through omaveloxolone’s 2023 approval as the first FDA-approved Nrf2/antioxidant pathway modulator in a neurodegenerative indication. Established CSF oxidative stress biomarkers and the post-Aduhelm/Leqembi clinical endpoint framework support development feasibility. Primary risks are antioxidant-specific clinical effect size adequacy and PRDX1 family redundancy/selectivity questions. Pre-IND meeting strongly recommended to align endpoint package and address selectivity preclinical strategy.
